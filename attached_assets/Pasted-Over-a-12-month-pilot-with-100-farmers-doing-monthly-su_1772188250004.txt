Over a 12-month pilot with 100 farmers doing monthly submissions, that's ~1,200 records. After 3 years with 500 farmers, it's ~18,000 records — each with GPS coordinates, time series, and verifier-confirmed labels. That dataset doesn't exist for Philippine bamboo anywhere.
Three model targets, in priority order
Target 1 — Species identification (near-term, buildable now)
Narrow problem: distinguish D. asper, B. blumeana (Tinik), and Guadua from a photo. Only 3 classes, not 1,600. Start with the MDPI paper's approach — fine-tune VGG16 or ResNet on our collected images once we have 200+ labeled examples per species. Roboflow is a good labeling and training infrastructure for this stage.
Target 2 — Age / maturity classification (medium-term)
Given a photo + known planting date, estimate age class (0–2 years, 2–4 years, 4+ years). This is a regression problem on image features correlated with known age. The temporal sequences TerraTwin naturally collects — same plot photographed monthly — are perfect training data for this. No other dataset has this.
Target 3 — Biomass proxy estimation (longer-term, the prize)
Derive a carbon biomass estimate from image features: culm diameter visible in frame, clump density, canopy coverage. Combine with allometric equations from Philippine DENR research. This requires the most data but unlocks model-based MRV — verification without a human auditor.
The key insight: Tinik is your baseline, not your target
B. blumeana (Tinik) grows wild everywhere already. Most farmer submissions in the early pilot will be Tinik plots. This is actually useful — Tinik becomes your negative class / baseline, and every confirmed D. asper or Guadua submission is a high-value positive label. The incentive structure (higher payments for D. asper) naturally drives toward the species you want labeled most.
